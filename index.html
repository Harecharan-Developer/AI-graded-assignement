Telerobotics

What is telerobotics

Initial Analysis of the word conveys us its literla meaning i.e, Tele- means remote and Robotics- mean the field of engineering and computer science that involves the design, construction, operation, and application of robots.

The official definition from cambridge is the study and use of robots (= machines that can perform complicated actions automatically) that are controlled from a distance:

Telerobotics involves controlling robots remotely over a distance, combining teleoperation, robotics, and communication technology for tasks in hazardous or unsuitable environments.

Use Cases

Places where telerobotics are used

  • Space Exploration:

    Robots like Mars rovers are controlled remotely for planetary surface exploration.

  • Medicine:

    Robotic surgical systems allow surgeons to perform minimally invasive procedures remotely

  • Manufacturing

    Telerobotic systems automate tasks in hazardous manufacturing environments.

  • Military

    Telerobotic systems are used for bomb disposal and other dangerous military tasks.

  • Agriculture

    Can be used to effectively deliver Pesticides

  • Disaster Response:

    Robots are deployed for search and rescue operations in disaster zones.

Latest Trends:

  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): Integration of AI improves efficiency and adaptability of telerobotic systems.
  • Internet of Things (IoT) Integration: Real-time data exchange and remote monitoring are enabled through IoT integration.
  • Human-Robot Collaboration: Research focuses on synergistic human-robot collaboration.

    Research Gaps

    Challenges in telerobotics research include:

  • First and foremost: Not many universities have a course or having a interest in teleoperating
  • Latency and Bandwidth: Ensuring real-time control and sufficient bandwidth for critical applications.
  • Safety and Security: Developing robust safety and cybersecurity measures.
  • Autonomy and Adaptability: Improving the ability of telerobotic systems to handle unforeseen circumstances.

    Virtual Reality

    Virtual Reality (VR) immerses users in computer-generated environments, providing interactive experiences and a sense of presence.

    Applications:

    • Gaming and Entertainment: Immersive gaming and virtual tours.
    • Education and Training: Realistic skill practice in simulations.
    • Architecture and Design: Visualization and interaction with 3D models.
    • Healthcare: Pain management, exposure therapy, and rehabilitation.
    Learn More

    Research Gaps:

    • Motion Sickness and Discomfort: Improving comfort and accessibility. Perceived motion can cause imbalance in a still world
    • Content Creation and Interactivity: Tools and techniques for creating compelling VR content.
    • Accessibility: Inclusive design and assistive technologies.
    • Ethical and Social Implications: Privacy, identity, and impact on real-world interactions.
